Riverine Heritage — On Review, Journal of Architecture BUET
Reactivating Riverine Heritage: Adaptive Reuse of the PS Lepcha
Bangladesh's paddle steamers, which represent a 150 year old riverine culture imparted since the British colonial period, saw the decline of their service since its suspension in 2022 owing to the Padma Bridge's interference with their passenger numbers and operational losses. Now obsolete vessels like the PS Lepcha, built in 1938 and idling in the Buriganga River, are bound for abandonment or scrapping, taking their maritime heritage down into the depths of history, serving highly placed personalities and communities in remote areas. This paper will be presenting an adaptive reuse intervention for the PS Lepcha so as not to allow its full abandonment, while transforming it into a vibrant cultural exchange hub that integrates local communities and attracts foreigners to the history of Old Dhaka. The goal is to transform PS Lepcha into a multifunctional space. This will include a museum of steam engines, a herbal tea lounge, meditation areas, existing cabins, a book café, and an art studio. The art studio will showcase rickshaw painting, a cultural art recognized by UNESCO that features Dhaka's unique style. Additionally, there will be a vendor space for artisans to sell their products and an amphitheater at the entry point with a jetty. Through qualitative methodologies, an assessment of the condition of PS Lepcha was conducted. In addition, global cases on vessel redevelopment were analyzed, followed by community engagement to establish community integration and needs, ensuring that cultural perspectives, such as those in the tradition of rickshaw art, are incorporated into the design. Analysis shows that PS Lepcha is feasible as an adaptable reuse cultural exchange platform, empowering the community through employment in tourism and art, as well as allowing foreign nationals to experience Dhaka’s vibrant history and urban experience. These projects ensure conservation of the cultural site with minimal handling. This article recommends that, in the same manner as the leasing scheme of the BIWTC for heritage cruises that is already in place, such projects should be prioritized over destruction through initiatives by the government.
Industrial Heritage — Thesis Research
From Mill to Memory: Jute Legacy Revival at Adamjee
The last surviving shed of Adamjee Jute Mill, once the world's largest, running 1950–2002 with 26,000 workers, now decays inside Narayanganj's AEPZ, its iron trusses and brick walls quietly erasing the marginalised labour history of the Bengali and Bihari community. This study supports the adaptive reuse of the site as an industrial museum and civic space. It follows the principles of critical heritage studies, the Venice Charter's approach of minimal intervention, and the Burra Charter's focus on living memory and community connection. A qualitative study through archival research, cross-comparison of the adaptive reuse strategies of Verdant Works (the Dundee jute industry revitalisation), Zollverein (the Essen UNESCO-listed industrial complex), and Imagine Studio (the Mumbai urban heritage weaving project), structural assessment of the site, and oral interviews with former Bihari and Bengali workers and BEPZA stakeholders affirm the viability of the site and the strong desire for spaces of inclusivity and recognition. The design reinvigorates a 50,000 sqm shed and 8,200 sqm precinct through six zones: a Machine Museum, a Sonali Bag Pavilion, a Workers' Memorial Garden, a Community Skills Courtyard, knowledge-sharing spaces, and a promenade along the riverfront. These interventions facilitate the inclusion of Bengali and Bihari populations and contribute to the achievement of SDG 8 (Decent Work), SDG 9 (Industry and Innovation), SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities), and SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption). Involving the community in industrial heritage, rather than relying on fixed exhibitions, can breathe new life into neglected locations and offer Bangladesh a blueprint for revitalising post-industrial areas.
Mosque Architecture — Istanbul Conference 2025
Mosque Morphosis: Forms and Openings in Dhaka's Community Mosques
Dhaka, known worldwide as ‘the City of Mosques’, has been closely associated with Islamic mosque architecture since its beginning. The city has a four hundred year history as Dhaka during which Islamic architecture has been designed and built in varying degrees during the several regimes and times. The Islamic architectural context of Dhaka has a distinguished place in the world. Dhaka is known for its mosques of all time. Now it’s experiencing a diversity of aesthetics in which every aspect of the mosque, from its shape to what is considered ‘typical’, has changed. The mosque is distinguished by its form at present, which relates to ventilation. Today, people are more interested in different shapes than the former typical shapes, and this is becoming more common. This study points to the differences and variations in form and ventilation designs between some of the former famous mosques and present mosques. In selecting mosques in the sample to examine, mosques of different characteristics, periods, and regimes have been prioritized. While investigating a number of mosques completed over the last few decades, the features, values, and trends that have shaped their architectural forms and spatial character are identified. As well as the literature review, the research dissects the form of the mosques from the mosque documentation and discovers ventilation performance through interviews with users. Decreasing the use of traditional doors and windows in design can facilitate ventilation in many ways. By exploring the rationality and acceptability of such opening forms, it is possibile to determine the reasons for selecting certain forms. With the information and data collected, the study explores how, and sometimes if, the forms and ventilation of mosques are changing, while maintaining an Islamic architectural style in Dhaka that is responsive to community needs. Today there are increased numbers of symbolic, aesthetic, attractive, and climate-responsive mosques in Dhaka that depart from the previous architectural style. However, architects must work to represent the fundamental needs of the mosque community while also addressing the fundamental spiritual aspects of mosque design.
Informal Settlement — IIT Roorkee 2026
Beyond Bulldozers: Participatory Upgrading in Tejgaon Kunipara
The informal settlement areas of Dhaka, Bangladesh, are facing various challenges affecting their daily lives. These challenges include the overcrowding of the living areas without proper ventilation. The roofs of the buildings are leaking. The buildings are not suitable for living. The areas are not well-sanitized. Flooding is common. There are no areas provided for the children and families. An example of an informal settlement area is Tejgaon Kunipara. It has 65,787 inhabitants living in 21,516 households across 5.5 hectares of land next to the rolling mills. It is noted for having 25 critical issues identified. In this research, an alternative approach to the conventional way of demolition and relocation is proposed. It is done by developing an upgrading framework for informal settlement areas through a collaborative approach with the inhabitants. It is done by using a mixed approach of five stages: site assessment, discussions and conducting focus groups among more than 200 families, comparative case studies of Khuda Ki Basti informal settlement areas of Karachi and Jhawtola Sebok Colony of Bangladesh, iterative design development, and validation of the designs by presenting the models to the inhabitants. The design prototypes focus on semi-pucca and kancha typologies, as well as bazaar and garage/workshop spaces, utilizing scrap steel sheets, frames, angles, and bamboo, which are readily available at various rolling mills in the region. This approach not only lowers construction expenses by 40-60% but also complies with the Bangladesh National Building Code. It provides more living space, better interior temperature control through ventilation, foundations against flooding, improved safety features in staircases and pathways, personal kitchens and bathrooms, and new spaces without losing existing residents. This process has been proven to meet requirements under UN-Habitat guidelines on incremental housing and SDG 11. Design that prioritizes people and their needs can strengthen social connections, optimize resources, and enhance social networks. This methodology provides a viable alternative to the destructive practice of clearing informal settlements in Dhaka City and other areas of the Global South. It transforms industrial waste into housing opportunities and enables residents to take control of their communities and build a sustainable future.
